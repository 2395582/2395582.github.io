What is Interaction for Data Visualization? - A Close Reading

INTRODUCTION

The papers introduction provides the ‘call to action’ which drives the main question behind the paper. The call to action being to enrich interactivity in data visualization systems, in an attempt to give more freedom to end users in a variety of ways.

This call to action, driven by the forward thinking of those in the data visualizations game, can immediately be seen as an important and highly necessary one. To ensure the best possible experience for those that are eventually going to use these systems (or any system) is a core facet of design. To, as a designer, shoulder on as much responsibility as possible, so that users are able to take full advantage of your designed systems when interacting with them.

Naturally this type of system optimization does not come without difficulties. (Trust me, I know). Whether some of these challenges are technical in nature, a core challenge lies in the definition of interaction. While designers can see the need for it, it is difficult to implement unless an understanding is come to. An understanding which is impossible to achieve without starting with a definition. What is interaction in data visualization?

REVIEWS & RATIONALES

The next section of the paper, goes in depth on the methods the writers used when researching this paper, in an attempt to come to an answer to the question.

This portion is very important academically, but it’s no Stephen King.

Rest assured, all the research was done in a very methodological manner in order to ensure the best results.

RESULTS

The intensive research undertaken by the writers, helped them to find common similarities between the definitions for interaction, as found across some of the papers.

It was then understood that there were certain mandatory components which would be necessary in order to define something as being interactive. These components are: External entities and actions, and Internal entities and actions.

EXTERNAL ENTITIES

Of the two most cited, are the user and the data. The user refers to the human being, equipped with the ability and motivation to interact with the system. (Dimara & Perin, 2020, p. 3).

The data refers to “an intangible information source that is the user’s main object of interest.” (Dimara & Perin, 2020, p. 3).

These first two building blocks of the definition are quite self-explanatory. Data visualization systems, are systems that attempt to visualize “intangible information” for a user equipped with sufficient “motivation” to peruse this data. These are the two external entities as they lay, ripe for interaction. The paper also mentions others such as time, but for this close reading I believe these two will suffice for now.

INTERNAL ENTITIES

Here the paper focuses on intent.

“Interaction is characterized as a goal-oriented activity involving a data-oriented intent.” (Dimara & Perin, 2020, p. 3).

Intention in interaction can range from a number of different ways that someone may use the data, for any number of reasons.

EXTERNAL ACTIONS

Here the paper describes the relationship of the interaction.

It is a dialogue of sorts. The user performs an action, and the system provides an (appropriate) response to said action.

The two external entities mentioned, the user and the data, partake in this relationship in order to warrant a definition of interaction. The user utilizes physical means of interaction, to prompt an appropriate and expected response from the system… if all went well.

INTERNAL ACTIONS

Besides the surface level means of interaction mentioned above, there is a deeper process being undertaken as well.

The user interacts on a cognitive level with the data, and the data responds in a like manner, be it through a reasoning or analytic response.

A similar action as its external counterpart, but on a more… profound level. (Normie plebs push buttons, Internal Actions understand on a deeper level)

WHAT INTERACTION FOR VISUALZATION CAN INVOLVE

Along with the afore mentioned entities and actions, specialized interaction for visualization can involve additional entities and actions.

These additions are as follows:

For External Entities, interaction can involve the physical real-world components of interaction, such as the physical tools (mouse, keyboard, etc.) as well as the physical appendages of the one who uses these tools (hands, eyes, etc.).

The paper warns that overlooking or restricting these modalities can be detrimental to the system, implying that taking advantage of them will lead to better use of capturing user intent.

For Internal Entities, the user is considered on a deeper level, taking into account any prior knowledge and experience they might have.

For External Actions, the user actions that might change the data are taken into account. The physical changes are classified as the user manipulating the data to a specific end.

Also considered is the dialogue between users and their peers. (“Hey Joe, seen the data last night?”)

For Internal Actions, the fact that the user interacts with the data on a much deeper cognitive level is once again considered, albeit in a much deeper way.

PROPERTIES OF INTERACTION

The paper now provides three properties of interaction for your consideration.

Granularity. Interactivity is described at multiple levels, from micro to macro and anything in between.

Continuous and Discreet interactions. “Continuous interactions involve a sequence of intermediate visualization states between an initial state and a goal state” while “With discrete interactions, action and reaction occur in a distinct manner” (Dimara & Perin, 2020, p. 5)

ACADEMICS

The paper then goes on to give a variety of information, in an attempt to bring the reader to the definition we seek. As I am fast running out of word count, I will attempt to summarize the key points in the following sections, as I have identified them.

The first key point, being the way Visualization conceptualizes interaction. It does this in a three-pronged approach. System, Task, and Human.

System approaches, focus on the system, through the use of skilled operators. At this level, there is no clarification of user/user-intentions.

Task-centric approaches, focus on interactions in terms of low-level tasks, distinguishing between data types, and user roles.

Human-centric approaches, focus on user intent on a deeper level.

The paper then attempts to find similarities and differences in interaction in visualization and interaction in HCI (Human-Computer Interaction).

This begins with some concepts that characterize interactions in HCI.

Having come to an understanding of HCI interaction, the paper now uses this to extend the view of visualization through the lens of HCI.

It does this first in terms of user intent. In HCI, user intent is not specifically considered. Rather there are general tools provided to the user, in order to achieve any number of tasks.

Data visualization, is naturally more focused than this. User intent plays a large role, and has to be considered in order to provide the best possible tools.

The second extension comes in terms of user profiling. In HCI the user is assumed to be… a human. No other assumption is made, and the interactions provided reflect that. Broader means of interaction, offered to a broader possibility of interactor.

In data visualization, once again the user is considered with much more specificity. The population of interactors is much more expertly catered to.

The paper then goes on to define some differences between HCI and the topic of discussion.

CONCLUSION

The paper eventually comes to an answer to the proposed question.

“Interaction for visualization is the interplay between a person and a data interface involving a data-related intent, at least one action from the person and an interface reaction that is perceived as such.”

(Dimara & Perin, 2020, p. 8)

This definition is quite self-explanatory, but I will break it down as succinctly as possible.

Interplay

This refers to the actions undertaken between the two main external entities discovered above, the user and the data.

Intent

The user, as previously mentioned, has an intent when approaching the data.

At least one action

Semantics. In order for interaction to occur, there has to be a minimum of one action on the part of the user.

Reaction that is perceived as such

An appropriate reaction to the user’s action, that is identifiable as a reaction.
