You picked a fine time to leave me, Lucille



She sits at the bar. No ring. A patron swoops in. A tip of the hat and a line later, she laughs.

He points at an empty stool. She nods. He sits.

Another laugh. Another line. Another man bursts in.

His ring is still on.

He’s not laughing.

And he’s done with lines.


The Prison Visit



Sofia walks into the room and sits at the assigned seat, tucking her skirt under her legs. Her husband, Vinny is already waiting for her on the other side of the glass. He smiles broadly, speaking words she can’t hear. He picks up the phone on his side of the partition and motions for her to do the same. She wraps a handkerchief around the handle and brings the phone to her ear.

They talk, his smile always a bit broader than hers. He leans back in his chair and asks her something. She answers with a small nod, the corners of her mouth twitching. Tears well up in her eyes.

Vinny leans forward to console his wife. He says something, making her smile. She looks at him, tilting her head at a slight angle. Her free hand moves forward, meets the partition, then returns to her lap.

He asks a question. She composes herself and answers in a way akin to a waitress reciting back a large order. He nods then leans back again. He asks something else. Her free hand grabs a handful of skirt under the table. It appears as though the waitress has forgotten the sugar. He yells, then looks around over his shoulder. He leans in closer and lowers his voice. He speaks slowly. She nods frantically, prompting a tear to roll down her cheek. He sighs, then soothes her.

She smiles. He leans back and lights a cigarette. A guard cuts off his next sentence.

He blows smoke in the guard’s face and sends him walking. The guard is quickly replaced by the warden. He grabs Vinny’s arm.

Vinny stands up and shrugs the warden off. He turns back to his wife and says something. For the first time in their exchange his smile falters a bit. Sofia notices and stands up. She nods in response, tears now flowing freely down her face. Her final words are cut off as the warden slams the phone into the receiver. Her husband gives her a final wink and a smile, then he’s gone.

On An Evening in Roma



There’s a fountain in the middle of the plaza. The cobblestoned street is bustling with people, either ending their day or starting their night. There’s a cinema on one side of the plaza. A small, seemingly insignificant building with double doors on either side of it’s ticket booth. Doors that promise a few hours of escape for anyone willing to pay the price.

The marquee holds the name of tonight’s attraction, in black letters on a white wall. “Una Corda di Bugie Bianche”, or “A String of White Lies”.

By night, the sign had done it’s job. A crowd had assembled, then filtered through the double doors into the cinema. Maybe it was the attraction of a famous cast, the promise of a visionary director, or maybe it was just film for the sake of film.

The crowd becomes an audience as it sits down, ushered to their seats by an old man with a flashlight. Chatter is exchanged excitedly, then the lights go, and the screen lights up. All is still. The movie starts.

The faces of the crowd- no, the audience, is lit up by the screen. Cigarette smoke gathers in a cloud above their heads, snacks are chewed, and hands are held. The movie speaks and they respond, a laugh here, a gasp there, sometimes just a smile. Cinema, is a living thing.
