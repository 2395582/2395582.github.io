Data Visualization, Data Art, and their Strategies of Representation.

In this blog, we take a deep dive into the relationship between Data Visualization and Data Art, as well as looking into the strategies employed by these concepts in terms of representation. Having unnecessarily broken down my title (in an attempt to bolster my word count – sue me) it now occurs to me that an understanding of these two concepts needs to be reached in order to draw up a conception of the relationship they share.

Data Visualization

An overview of Li’s Overview of Data Visualization (2020), is already available in another blog, but it is the best place to look in our attempt to reach an understanding of Data Visualization. This is due to the fact that it gives several definitions from several scholars, which make the definition as clear and understandable as possible. One such definition, a personal favorite is Kirk’s (as quoted by Li).

(Data Visualization is…) the representation and presentation of data that exploits our visual perception abilities in order to amplify cognition.

Herein we find two things of note; a definition of data visualization, and an allusion as to how data visualization works. While the ‘how’ of the matter has only been loosely stated, a closer reading (so to speak) will inform us further.

The representation and presentation of data, refers to the fact that data vis deals with the portrayal of data, that is information. This data is portrayed in a visual form, (in this context achieved through software and computer nerds) and takes advantage of our inherent visual systems to reach an understanding. It is a deliberate means of getting a message across, through a visual medium, in a way that ensures the message is fully comprehensible.

Data visualizations, commonly in the form of graphs and charts, are used for analytical reasons. The data set, a complex collection of information, is represented in a way that is much more palatable through data vis. Not only is the information rendered easier to understand, but the data set can be used to ‘tell a story’. While the data set may have a myriad of possibilities when it comes to knowledge generation, the data visualization (in its limited capacity) shows the parts of that data that are deemed most necessary to achieve comprehension on a specific point.

“We need to think more about the implications of what is shown within such graphics, and what remains missing”

(Kidd, 2015)

This brings us to the representation strategies of data vis. As Kidd posits in the aforementioned quote, data visualizations have an inherently reductive quality. The data vis, dependant on the data set, only shows what they want you to see. It is a manipulation of the data, and by extension a manipulation of the purveyor of the data vis, in order to attain full comprehension on the point of interest. Now said point of interest may just be the focus of the project at hand, but it could also be used pull the proverbial wool over peoples eyes, in an attempt to gain control of the system of knowledge generation, if not intentionally, then through the reductive nature of the system. The strategy of representation thus becomes a dangerous one, which must be manipulated with care, and whose dangerous by-products cannot be avoided.

Data Art.

If data visualization is a raincoat, data art is that same raincoat with a brand-name tagged on. A matter of form and function. While this analogy may be a bit reductive, as data vis is also designed with form in mind to a great extent, in data art, that form is aestheticized.

“…artistic visualizations are visualizations of data done by artists with the intent of making art.”

(Viégas, et al. 2007)

Similar to data vis, data art begins with the humble data set. This data set, and the information stored within it, are used to make artistic visualizations which simultaneously represent the information, and (hopefully) enhance it in an artistic way. Unlike data vis which focuses on attaining an amplified cognition, or understanding of the information, data art focuses on representing the data in a novel manner. While originality is important in this (as in all other artistic endeavours) it must be considered that true creative spirit should manage to create that which is both novel and useful. Data vis errs on the side of useful and data art floats closer to the centre of this spectrum.

If we assume data art to fall under the category of techno art (which is a safe assumption to make based on Roeser’s definitions (2018)) then we can conclude that techno art (and by extension data art) can help people make:

“…abstract problems concrete, explore new scenarios, challenge their imaginations, and broaden their narrow personal perspectives through empathy, sympathy and compassion.”

(Roeser, 2018)

This now spills into the representational strategies of data art. Similar to data vis, data art can be assumed to fall prey to the same sort of reductive nature mentioned earlier, but its representational falls further into what Kidd (2015) would refer to as ‘persuasion’.

In Kidd’s analysis of the relationship between representation and persuasion, the article breaks it down in terms of advertisements. Data art, I would argue (backed by Roeser’s emphasis on the use of emotions through art, a truism as far as I’m concerned) could, and probably would participate in a similar representational strategy of persuasion. By moulding the data set into something quite unrecognizable, and also aesthetically pleasing, the raw information is undeniably tempered. In the name of art, yes. In the name of amplifying cognition while pleasing the eye, yes. But tempered with, nonetheless. It not only reduces the data set to appear in a simpler form (like in the case of data vis) but also configures it in the name of art.

While data art may not be celebrated for its analytical purposes in more serious circles, the ever-expanding reach of data art poses an ever-growing problem; how much will it sway people with art based on facts? What powers does it hold in terms of persuasion? And perhaps most importantly, is it possible to create data art, without resorting to these manipulative representational strategies?
