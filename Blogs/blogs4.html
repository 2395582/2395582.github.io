The World WIDE Web, you say?

Toward a study of information geographies

The article deals with a rather bleak concept, but a concept which holds a great importance, particularly for those directly involved in the distribution of information. The concept of Information Geographies.

INTRODUCTION

The distribution of information is a key role in the advancement of mankind. To grow, we must learn, and to learn we must have that which can be learned, that is, information. It is an invaluable currency in this world and like any other currency which is valuable beyond words it is subject to an undeniably unfair distribution.

This creates a power hierarchy; those who control information, that is, the creation and distribution of information, are in power. They may, unwittingly, withhold information to certain places, or fail to represent certain places. If information is not given about a certain place, that place might as well be non-existent. Perhaps that’s a bit of an extreme statement, but I do believe it holds some truth.

PRE-DIGITAL

The concept of information geographies is by no means restricted to digital means of information sharing. The technological advancements made before the advent of digital technology as we’ve come to know it (and rely on to a worrying degree), also created a disparage of information sharing. The printing press made the release of widespread information possible, but the spread was more akin to that of a flamethrower as opposed to the wildfire of information that should’ve scorched the earth, worldwide. Books, newspapers, and other such media were made for and in the global North. Countries with widespread information, remained informed, about themselves. While countries lacking said spread of information, remained ill-informed and underrepresented. As Mark Twain once said “If you don’t read the newspaper, you are uninformed. If you do read it, you are misinformed.” What if you don’t even get the chance to read the newspaper?

STATISTICS

The article then goes on to demonstrate the information geographies, through a number of statistics. As with most statistics there are some outliers, but a definite pattern arises of a lower number of information sources available in certain geographical locations, (particularly those in the global South). Thus, the article shows how those countries which have previously fell behind in terms of information gathering, continue to fall behind. The work of the article is divided into three categories: namely access, participation, and representation.

ACCESS

Is the first step of getting to a solution for this problem. If more locations can get access to the technologies necessary to take part in the sharing of information, then this will, in time, increase the likelihood of a surge in both representation and participation.

PARTICIPATION

The paper now breaks down the level of participation globally across three different avenues: domain names, Github repos and Wikipedia edits. The stats collected on the participation of people across the world on these three bases provides another look at the disparage that is apparent. Certain countries have a strong presence across the board, while for others, the opposite is true.

REPRESENTATION

The section on representation shows the continuous pattern of unevenness through the different platforms that facilitate the representation of different geographical locations. “Although each platform is characterised by its own idiosyncrasies, some parts of the world are massively over-represented (North America and Europe) and the rest of the world is severely under-represented.”

CONCLUSION

The results of the research done in the article are rather bleak. It is a tough pill to swallow perhaps, but relief can only be found in taking the medicine. This brings to the realisation that a definite effort must be made to increase the three above mentioned facets on a wide geographical scale.
